# Significance and Implications

The central finding in this capsule is not merely that measurement noise degrades a metric. It is that, across multiple studied families, the observed sensor-side error can become directionally wrong before the true performance ranking changes sign. In the original explicit plant-plus-PI probe, the `zeta = 0.15` versus `zeta = 0.707` pair shows an observed crossover at about `0.106`, while the true pairwise crossover arrives later at about `0.124`. The newer replications extend that pattern: a second explicit PID family shows the same directional inversion in two pairs, a domain-specific power-plant load-following family reproduces it again in its strongest pair, and a simplified missile-guidance / autopilot family now reproduces it in two pairs. There is therefore a real inversion window in which a design-comparison procedure based on the sensor-side error would recommend the wrong controller even though the cleaner design still has lower true cost.

That distinction matters because it changes the level of seriousness. Ordinary noise sensitivity means the metric becomes less reliable. Occupancy inversion means the metric can become adversarial for design choice. In that regime, an engineer is not simply working with a noisy estimate of the truth. The engineer is receiving a decision signal whose sign has flipped. This makes the phenomenon potentially important for real control-system evaluation workflows, because many comparison and tuning procedures implicitly trust the measured error signal as the thing to minimize.

The result is also sharper than a generic warning about high-memory designs. Not every pair in every family inverts. That means the effect is structured rather than universal. The current evidence points to a pair-specific threshold governed by the interaction between clean-regime advantage and noise-loaded excess penalty, not just by noise level alone. The most useful threshold variable in this study remains the pairwise excess-penalty parity ratio, and across the current positive pairs it clusters tightly around the observed crossover, typically in the `0.85` to `0.90` range, before full parity is reached.

Because the pattern now holds in multiple explicit families, a domain-specific load-following architecture, and a high-stakes guidance-style architecture, the implication for the domain is more substantial than it was in the first probe. Some current controller-comparison practices appear vulnerable to a hidden failure mode: the very error metric used to choose between designs can stop preserving the true ranking in the noise regime where discrimination matters most. That does not imply that classical control is wrong, nor that all deployed systems are compromised. It does imply that some evaluation and tuning procedures may need a two-regime protocol. Below the inversion threshold, the observed sensor-side error remains usable for ranking designs. Above it, system-side diagnostics such as low-band or shadow-based metrics may be more trustworthy because they are upstream of the noise recycling that corrupts the observed error.

This is why the finding feels consequential. It is not only a new metric story. It is a candidate diagnostic deficiency in existing practice. If a controller can be rejected because the measurement channel has made the comparison metric directionally misleading, then the problem is not just estimation quality. It is decision quality. The new guidance-style replication makes that stake more concrete. In high-stakes domains such as guidance, autopilot design, process control, or other long-horizon feedback systems, that possibility alone is enough to justify deeper validation.
