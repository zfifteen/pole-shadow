# Shadow Mass Saturation Threshold

A long pole shadow improves slow-input tracking only until the environment injects enough nuisance energy for that extra persistence to become costly. Below that point, lightly damped designs retain enough temporal structure to outperform more heavily damped ones on slow signals. Beyond it, additional shadow mass stops acting like useful memory and starts acting like a reservoir for nuisance energy, so the best-performing design shifts toward a smaller but still nonzero shadow mass.

This is not a claim that textbook-robust damping suddenly becomes optimal. In the current dedicated study, the clean optimum occurs at zeta = 0.1 for both command-side and measurement-side nuisance ladders. As the environment worsens, the preferred design moves inward: to zeta = 0.25 under the heaviest command-side nuisance tested, and to zeta = 0.3 under the heaviest measurement-side nuisance tested. The important point is the existence of a moving interior optimum, not a wholesale reversal in favor of high damping.

The phenomenon is easy to miss because damping ratio and stability margin are only indirect proxies for it. In a simple one-parameter family they move monotonically with shadow mass, but they do not identify the noise-conditioned sweet spot itself. In the current study, a simple occupancy-style proxy, `noise_power * shadow_mass_l2`, tracks excess slow-tracking penalty more clearly than noise level alone and more directly than damping ratio as a cross-environment descriptor. What matters is not just how much shadow mass the system has, but how much nuisance energy the environment can load into that mass over the observation window.

This suggests a sharper design principle: choose shadow mass to match the environment rather than maximizing or minimizing it blindly. Too little shadow mass produces temporal myopia and poor slow tracking. Too much shadow mass allows nuisance energy to persist long enough to erode the very benefit that extra memory was supposed to provide. The relevant question is therefore not "how lightly damped can I make the system?" but "how much shadow mass can this environment afford?"

The practical implication is that every operating regime may induce a preferred shadow-mass budget. Clean environments reward longer shadows. Noisier environments favor an interior optimum that depends on both the entry point and the strength of the nuisance. The engineering task is therefore not to push poles as close as possible to the imaginary axis, but to allocate just enough shadow mass to preserve slow structure without saturating the system with persistent noise.
