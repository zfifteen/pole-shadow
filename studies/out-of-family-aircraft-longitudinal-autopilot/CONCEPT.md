# Aircraft Autopilot Boundary Case

A domain-specific aircraft autopilot preserves the pole-shadow framework in a weaker but still important form. In the current modified F-8 longitudinal study, lower-damping designs still perform better than a more textbook `zeta = 0.707` design on the slow glide-profile mission, even though the comparison is now embedded in a realistic cascaded elevator autopilot rather than a hand-shaped closed-loop template.

The main difference is that the aircraft family does not reproduce the full moving-optimum story seen in the plant-plus-PI and shadow-mass studies. In this run, the clean optimum is `zeta = 0.2`, and it remains `zeta = 0.2` across the tested command-side and measurement-side nuisance ladders. The result is therefore not a domain-specific phase transition, but a domain-specific boundary case: the framework still exposes slow-mission structure, but the best aircraft tuning does not migrate across the tested regimes.

This makes the transient-summary question more subtle. Pitch-step settling time is not blind in this family the way it was in the stronger matched-settling studies; it still correlates strongly with mission cost. But it does not tell the whole story either. Near-matched pitch transients can still hide meaningful glide-profile penalties, and slow-band deficit ranks the family more cleanly than pitch settling does. The practical lesson is that a realistic autopilot can compress the hidden-cost story without eliminating it.

The occupancy-style proxy is also weaker here. `noise_power * shadow_mass_l2` tracks excess glide-profile penalty, but not by much more than nuisance power alone. That matters because it prevents the project from overclaiming. The aircraft result says the framework is not a one-size-fits-all replacement for classical control summaries. It is a new diagnostic layer whose strength depends on the domain, the mission geometry, and the way nuisance enters the loop.

The practical implication is still significant. Even in this narrower aircraft setting, a more robust-looking damping choice does not automatically produce better slow-mission tracking. The engineering task is therefore not just to certify a clean pitch transient, but to ask whether the autopilot is preserving enough low-frequency mission structure over the full flight task. This study supports that diagnostic question while also marking a clear limit on how far the current stronger claims can be generalized.
