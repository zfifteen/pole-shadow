# Feedback Measurement-Noise Phase Transition

When measurement noise enters the feedback path, the problem changes in a deeper way than a noisy rollout would suggest. The issue is not only that the metric becomes harder to read. The issue is that the identity of the best design can change. A controller that is genuinely best for clean slow tracking can stop being best once sensor noise is strong enough to circulate through the loop, and that shift can happen before ordinary observed error metrics make the transition obvious.

In the current matched-settling study, the closed-loop second-order models are interpreted as unity-feedback complementary sensitivities, so sensor noise enters through `y = T(r - n)`. Under clean conditions, the lowest tested damping ratio, `zeta = 0.15`, is the best design for slow tracking. With modest measurement noise, the optimum shifts upward to `zeta = 0.25`. By noise levels `0.08` and above, the best design shifts again to `zeta = 0.4`, where it remains through the highest tested noise regimes. This is not a reversal in favor of textbook-robust damping. It is a movement toward an interior optimum.

That result sharpens the earlier settling-time blind-spot story. Slow-band deficit is still an excellent detector of clean low-frequency tracking liability. In the clean regime, its rank fidelity to true slow-tracking cost is perfect, while step settling time performs poorly on the same matched-settling family. But once measurement noise becomes strong enough to reshape the objective itself, clean-environment low-band metrics stop being sufficient. They are not failing because they were meaningless. They are failing because the environment has changed what "best" means.

The follow-up experiment also exposes a second blind spot. Even after the true optimum begins to move, the sensor-side view of performance can lag behind that shift or misreport it entirely. For the pair `zeta = 0.15` versus `zeta = 0.707`, the lower-damping design still wins on true tracking cost with probability about `0.93` at measurement-noise level `0.12`, yet the observed sensor-side error metric reports it as the winner only about `0.17` of the time. By noise level `0.20`, the pair `zeta = 0.25` versus `zeta = 0.707` still favors `zeta = 0.25` on true cost in most trials, while the observed metric makes it appear inferior. In other words, the measurement channel can hide the transition even after the plant-side truth has begun to move.

The practical implication is that a clean-environment diagnostic and a noisy-environment diagnostic are not the same thing. Settling time remains a weak detector of clean slow-tracking liability. Slow-band deficit reveals that liability well, but it does not by itself predict the new optimum once measurement noise becomes a first-class part of the objective. A complete design rule therefore has to do two things at once: quantify low-band temporal competence under clean conditions, and quantify how much sensor noise the loop will recycle before that competence turns into a liability.

This suggests a broader design principle. The engineer should not ask only, "How much temporal memory does this system preserve?" They should also ask, "How expensive does that memory become once the sensor stops being quiet?" The answer is not a single universal best damping ratio. It is a noise-conditioned operating curve whose optimum can move well before classical step summaries or naive rollout metrics reveal that it has moved.
